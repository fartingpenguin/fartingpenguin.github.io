Why are hawker centres so important to Singapore’s culture/identity?

Hawker centres are very important to Singapore’s culture because it is a huge part of the way of life for Singaporeans. Hawker centres are where people from all walks of life gather at hawker centres to eat and bond over their favourite hawker food. In addition, many of the hawker dishes originated from the food cultures of different immigrant groups who settled in Singapore. In addition, hawker centres have been around for a long time, and they are one of the few things that have not changed in Singapore’s evolution in the past years. Some of the oldest hawkers today have started their practice since the 1960s. A survey conducted by the National Environment Agency in 2016 concluded that 9 in 10 respondents agreed strongly that hawker centres are an important part of Singapore’s identity. In addition, Singapore voted Hawker Centres for the UNESCO’s Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.

How has Covid affected Hawkers?

The value of the vast majority of prepared food stalls has decreased in FY2021, owing to the impact of COVID-19. The leases of 341 prepared food stallholders were available for renewal between April and June 2021, following a rent freeze from April 2020 to March 2021. Their rents were reduced to the market rate in 58 percent of the cases. This group's reductions varied from $30 to more than $2,500. 37 percent continue to pay the same rent as they did during their previous tenancy. 

Madam Elaine Tay, 44, was shopping for her family's dinner. "Some of the stalls I usually patronise are closed today, so I'm buying from what is open," the marketing executive said. "I understand that maybe some of the stalls are worried, but honestly, it is so tiring to keep hearing about hawker centres closing for disinfection." The Jalan Besar Town Council told ST on Thursday : "The safety of our stallholders, residents and the public remains our top priority. We will take all necessary precautions before safely reopening the premises." Due to Covid-19 transmission, other markets and hawker centres in Singapore have lately closed for disinfection.

How have hawkers evolved over the years?

In the 1990s, the Housing and Development Board would update the food centre and market on a regular basis to enhance the amenities. Fixed tables and chairs were erected in 1991. The centre installed a new sanitation system in 1996. The centre was then updated once again in 2006. The facility had almost 700 booths when it reopened in 2008. The upgrade cost about 20.8 million dollars. Today the Chinatown complex market and food centre has around 260 stalls.
